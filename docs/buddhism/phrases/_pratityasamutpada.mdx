### Pratītyasamutpāda Gāthā

<Phrase {...phrase} {...props} />

<details>
  <summary>
    Dependent origination can be contrasted with the classic Western concept of [causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality_(philosophy)) in which an action by one thing is said to cause a change in another thing.
    Dependent origination instead views the change as being caused by many factors, not just one or even a few.
  </summary>
  <div>
    {/* @ts-ignore */}
    > Those [dharmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abhidharma)
      which arise from a cause,
      the [Tathāgata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tath%C4%81gata)
      has declared their cause.<br />
    > And that which is the cessation of them, thus the great renunciant
      ([sramana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%9Arama%E1%B9%87a)) has taught.<br />
    > Through [ignorance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avidy%C4%81_(Buddhism)),
      [karma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karma_in_Buddhism) is accumulated,
      [karma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karma_in_Buddhism) is the cause of
      [(re)birth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebirth_(Buddhism)).<br />
    > Through [knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praj%C3%B1%C4%81_(Buddhism)),
      [karma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karma_in_Buddhism) is not accumulated,
      through absence of [karma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karma_in_Buddhism),
      one is not [(re)born](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebirth_(Buddhism)).

    The additional verse is [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia) specific
    and does not have an equivalent in any [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism) text,
    whether in the original [Indic language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_South_Asia#Geographical_distribution) or [Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_script) or [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language) translation.
