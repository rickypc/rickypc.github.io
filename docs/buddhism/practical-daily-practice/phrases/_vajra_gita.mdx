### Vajragītā

<Phrase {...phrase} {...props} />

<details>
  <summary>
    This dharmatā song evokes the wisdom of [Ādibuddha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adi-Buddha)
    in union and is revered in the [Dzogchen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dzogchen)
    tradition as the naturally resonant mantra of the primordial [Buddha
    Samantabhadra](https://www.rigpawiki.org/index.php?title=Buddha_Samantabhadra).
    Known as the Song of the Vajra ([Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_script): རྡོ་རྗེའི་གླུ),
    it originates from the *Tantra of the Union of the Sun and Moon* and
    appears in Longchenpa's *Treasury of the Supreme Vehicle* as a profound
    expression of the true nature of reality. Its arranged syllables are
    chanted to invoke the inseparable union of compassion and wisdom,
    symbolizing the primordial coalescence beyond duality and pointing directly
    to the awakened state.

    This union is traditionally understood as the primordial (or mystical)
    union of [compassion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karu%E1%B9%87%C4%81)
    and [wisdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wisdom_in_Buddhism), depicted as
    a male [deity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deity) in union with his
    female consort through the principles of
    [interpenetration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpenetration_(Buddhism))
    or `coalescence`[^1], often illustrated through the metaphor of [Indra's net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indra%27s_net).

    While the Song of the Vajra belongs to the Dzogchen tradition, its
    symbolism resonates with the union imagery of [Anuttarayoga tantra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anuttarayoga_tantra)
    and the interpretive methods of [twilight language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twilight_language).
    The masculine figure represents compassion ([karuṇā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karu%E1%B9%87%C4%81))
    and skillful means ([upāya-kauśalya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upaya)),
    while the feminine partner embodies `wisdom` ([prajñā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praj%C3%B1%C4%81_(Buddhism))).
    Their union symbolizes the realization that overcomes the veils of
    [Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_(illusion)) - the false duality
    of subject and object.
  </summary>
